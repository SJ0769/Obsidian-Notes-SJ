- **Logical Pluralism:** The view that multiple logics which are equally valid, logic is a theory of validity, that is, a study of valid inference from some given rules (internal to the logic). According to this philosophical view, other logics could hold in certain instances/contexts or other 'possible' worlds (i.e., see extended modal realism below). This results in the notion of 'truth' to be relative to the logic you are working in (i.e., logical relativism).
	- **Logical Monism:** The view that there is only a single logic, which holds in all circumstances. Philosophers of this view would argue that classical logic is inherent to his world (i.e., there are no contradictory observation in reality) and other logics cannot exist in reality
	- **Logical Nihilism:** The view that there is no 'logic' in any form and there are no laws of logics as all fail universally. This appears to delve into trivialism (on the other hand, this could be seen as an extreme form of logical pluralism or nihilism where there are trivialist logic, as trivialism can be formulated as a logic, see [[Trivialism]])

- **Modal realism** is the view that possible worlds as formalized in modal logic, actually exist and are equally 'real' as the 'actual' world (within this context our actual world as that is what possibility is being referred to relative to us).
	- These possible worlds are existentially (spatiotemporal) isolated and so you cannot interact or travel to another possible world (if you could then they would be the same possible world). Different from a multiverse or other cosmological structures.

- **(Extended) Modal Realism** is the view that impossible worlds  also exist, which allow contradictions. These worlds would operate on paraconsistent (and even fully trivialist) logics. 'Modality' is treated as a dimension similar to time & space (i.e., an object is not just extended in space & time, but also through possible and impossible worlds; some parts of it exist in the actual world and other parts in non-actual worlds, although technically they are all actual worlds).

**Determinism:** The view that all events (in particular, human actions like 'choices') are determined by past causes and certain laws of nature.
Another example is that we cannot "will what we will", that it, we have no control over what you desire (this idea is linked to Schopenhauer), it is easy to see that this will devolve into infinite descent where even if we could "will what we will", then we could not "will what we will to will."


**Compatibilism:** The view that free will exists and determinism are not mutually exclusive, and rather that they are compatible.
$\to$ The idea is that we still have a 'degree' of free will (an illusion of choice?) in that we can choose between many choices, although all of which have been casually determined.

Consider the following though experiment:

You go to an ice cream store which offers two flavors of ice cream; vanilla and chocolate. You have always preferred chocolate and brought up in an environment where everyone prefers chocolate, so obviously you choose chocolate and pay the store person $6. Now consider that you have a time machine and travel back to the very moment before you pay for ice cream (ignoring time paradoxes and the such) and decide this time to try vanilla as it is something different from the usual (this way you are effectively paying $6 for two ice creams). It can be seen that both decisions are already determined, the choice of chocolate from prior biases, and the choice of vanilla which was determined from your 'prior' choice of chocolate and the want to try something new. In this sense there is a fragment of free will preserved in that you can choose between options, but any chosen option has already been determined.


**Note:** Determinists that argue against compatibilism will often say that they are playing a semantics game and are redefining what it means to be 'free' (i.e., unbound from any external influence), in this sense determinism is correct. A compatibilist will then reply that the notion of free will which their discussing is a more intuitive one w/ what we actually mean when we say 'free.'

**Predeterminism:** Future events have already been predetermined i.e., you could look into the future and know w/ 100% accuracy that this will occur. Disagrees completely w/ the notion of 'free will' in that not even a fragment or 'illusion' is preserved.

**Idealism:** The view that reality is composed of ideas and is mental in nature, thus reality is dependent on the mind. This form of idealism, often called 'ontological idealism', does deny the existence of objects independent of the mind.
The view that reality (our perception of it) is constituted of ideas and knowledge is dependent of the mind.

Are all perspectives equally 'real'?
	**Berkeley's Idealism:** What defines an interpretation of something is if it is an idea in the mind of god.
	**Descartes Idealism:** What defines a 'real' interpretation of something is an idea in the collective consciousness
Berkeley's view has some issues in particular w/ collapsing the distinction between an idea and the act of 'perceiving' something, which is also treated as an idea (i.e., "of the mind").

**Transcendental Idealism:** Transcendental Idealism (also called 'epistemological idealism'), is the view that our perception of reality and knowledge of it are mind dependent. In this view we do not perceive reality directly but rather our minds structure interprets it. Transcendental idealists do not deny the existence of an external reality independent to us, but rather say as Kant has put it, that that the intrinsic nature of physical objects - the "thing in itself" (noumena) - are fundamentally unknowable. Kant's particular form of transcendental idealism goes further asserting that space & time are a priori knowledge through which we structure experience.

**Note:** A transcendental idealist will argue that our sense data are an approximation of reality, but we can never fully gleam the total knowledge about an object (i.e., sense data may be just illusions produced by noumena).

There are issues w/ noumena being fundamentally ineffable or inaccessible to us as it could then not be stated logically.

A priori knowledge is knowledge that can be understood w/out experience, a subset of this is knowledge which can derived from purely logical processes. 'Intuitive' knowledge is our direct/immediate knowledge of truths (i.e., something asserted as a proposition). A lot of intuitive knowledge is a priori, although not all.
